Mukago (n.)
From omukago — Luganda for the pact.

In the kingdom of Buganda, two people who chose to become kin would share a single coffee bean — each swallowing half. A bond taken in, impossible to give back. We named our coffee after the promise.

Why Mukago exists.

The people who grow coffee see the least of what it earns. In Uganda, a farmer is paid in coins per kilo; in Britain the same kilo sells for pounds. The chain is long, and the grower stands at the wrong end of it.

Mukago is what happens when the farmer refuses to let go of the bean.

I grow it. I dry it. I ship it. I sell it to you.

One pair of hands the whole way — so what the coffee earns stays where the coffee grows.

I'm Ibrahim. On weekdays I build software in England. The rest of me is planting land in Kangulumira.

The making of a pact.

Every bag is built the way a pact is made: two halves, one promise, impossible to undo.
